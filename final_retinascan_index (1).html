HOW IT WORKS

A learning loop that mirrors clinical practice.

01

Choose a Case

Browse 50 authenticated clinical fundus photographs across 10 conditions. Filter by difficulty — Beginner, Intermediate, or Advanced. Each case includes the high-resolution fundus image, AI-generated teaching notes, and key biomarkers to identify.

02

Analyze with AI Guidance

Work through a guided diagnostic pathway. The AI overlay highlights regions of interest using Grad-CAM heatmaps. Identify biomarkers, note the disc, vessels, and macula, and form your differential diagnosis.

03

Submit Your Diagnosis

Enter your top condition, rate your confidence level, and submit. The system compares your answer to the AI's structured output and scores your accuracy, specificity, and calibration.

04

Review, Track, Repeat

Get immediate feedback with clinical explanations. Your results feed into your dashboard — updating your accuracy score, confidence calibration, and adaptive case recommendations. Complete quizzes to reinforce your learning.

CLINICAL REFERENCE

Condition Reference Guide

Clinical descriptions, key signs, and AI-detectable biomarkers for each condition in the dataset.

Healthy Fundus

Baseline reference — normal retinal anatomy
Key signs: Normal cup-to-disc ratio (0.2–0.4), clear foveal reflex, pink retina, normal vascular caliber, no arteriovenous nicking, symmetric optic discs.
Normal C/D ratio Clear fovea Normal vessels
5 cases available: 5 Beginner

Diabetic Retinopathy

~34% of people with diabetes develop DR · Leading cause of blindness in working-age adults
Key signs: Microaneurysms (small red dots), dot-blot hemorrhages, flame hemorrhages, hard exudates (yellow lipid deposits), cotton-wool spots, venous beading, intraretinal microvascular abnormalities (IRMA), neovascularization (proliferative stage).
Progression: Mild NPDR → Moderate NPDR → Severe NPDR → Proliferative DR.
Microaneurysms Hard exudates Hemorrhages Neovascularization IRMA
12 cases available: 4 Beginner · 5 Intermediate · 3 Advanced

Glaucoma

~3% of adults over 40 · Second leading cause of irreversible blindness worldwide
Key signs: Enlarged cup-to-disc ratio (vertical cupping), neuroretinal rim thinning, RNFL wedge defects, disc hemorrhages (Drance hemorrhage), peripapillary atrophy (beta zone), asymmetric C/D ratio between eyes.
Progression: Glaucoma suspect → Early → Moderate → Advanced (near-total cupping).
Vertical cupping Rim thinning RNFL defect Disc hemorrhage
9 cases available: 3 Beginner · 4 Intermediate · 2 Advanced

Hypertensive Retinopathy

~2–17% of non-diabetic adults · Reflects systemic vascular disease
Key signs: Generalized arteriolar narrowing, focal arteriolar constriction, arteriovenous (AV) nicking, copper/silver-wire arterioles, flame hemorrhages, cotton-wool spots, optic disc edema (malignant hypertension).
Grading: Keith-Wagener-Barker Grade 1 → 2 → 3 → 4.
AV nicking Arteriolar narrowing Flame hemorrhages Cotton-wool spots
6 cases available: 2 Beginner · 3 Intermediate · 1 Advanced

Age-Related Macular Degeneration

~11% of adults over 65 · Leading cause of central vision loss in older adults
Key signs: Drusen deposits (small yellow deposits), retinal pigment epithelial (RPE) changes, geographic atrophy (dry AMD), choroidal neovascularization and subretinal fluid (wet AMD), disciform scar (end-stage).
Progression: Early (small drusen) → Intermediate (large drusen, pigment changes) → Advanced (geographic atrophy or neovascular AMD).
Drusen RPE changes Geographic atrophy CNV
7 cases available: 2 Beginner · 3 Intermediate · 2 Advanced

Retinal Vein Occlusion

~0.5–2% of adults over 40 · Second most common retinal vascular disease
Key signs: Flame-shaped and dot-blot hemorrhages (sectoral in BRVO, all quadrants in CRVO), cotton-wool spots, dilated tortuous veins, disc edema, macular edema, retinal ischemia.
Types: Branch RVO (BRVO) · Central RVO (CRVO) · Hemi-retinal VO.
Flame hemorrhages Dilated veins Cotton-wool spots Disc edema
5 cases available: 1 Beginner · 2 Intermediate · 2 Advanced

Artery Occlusion

~1 in 10,000 · Ophthalmologic emergency requiring urgent workup
Key signs: Cherry-red spot at the fovea (CRAO), ischemic retinal whitening/pallor, box-carring of blood column in arterioles, attenuated arterioles, cotton-wool spots, sectoral pallor in BRAO.
Types: Central RAO (CRAO) · Branch RAO (BRAO) · Ophthalmic artery occlusion.
Cherry-red spot Retinal pallor Arteriolar attenuation
4 cases available: 1 Beginner · 1 Intermediate · 2 Advanced

Inflammatory / Vasculitic

Variable prevalence · Associated with systemic autoimmune conditions
Key signs: Perivascular sheathing (candle-wax drippings), retinal vasculitis, vitreous cells and haze, disc edema, macular edema, retinal infiltrates, peripheral retinal ischemia.
Associations: Sarcoidosis, Behçet disease, lupus, multiple sclerosis, tuberculosis.
Perivascular sheathing Vasculitis Vitreous cells
3 cases available: 1 Beginner · 1 Intermediate · 1 Advanced

Other Pathology

Miscellaneous conditions not in primary categories
Includes: Epiretinal membrane (wrinkling of inner retina), optic disc drusen (buried drusen, anomalous elevation), myopic degeneration, macular hole, retinal detachment signs, and other uncommon conditions.
ERM Disc drusen Myopic changes
3 cases available: 1 Beginner · 1 Intermediate · 1 Advanced

No Clear Disease

Diagnostic specificity training — challenging normal variants
Purpose: Cases with normal fundus appearance, imaging artifacts, or inconclusive findings. Essential for calibrating diagnostic specificity — not every fundus has pathology. Teaches students to avoid false-positive diagnoses.
Normal variant Artifact recognition
1 case available: 1 Advanced
